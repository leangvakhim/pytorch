Step 1: The Last Gear

$\frac{\partial f}{\partial q} = 1$

Autograd starts at the very end and moves one step backward.

Equation: $$f = q + z$$

It asks: "If I add exactly 1 to $q$, what happens to $f$?"

Let's test it: Since $q=6$ and $z=4$, currently $f=10$. If we bump $q$ up to $7$, the new math is $7+4 = 11$.

The answer went up by exactly 1. Because this is simple addition, Gear Q and Gear F turn at the exact same speed (a 1:1 ratio).

Step 2: The Multiplier Gear

$\frac{\partial q}{\partial x} = y = 3$

Now Autograd takes another step back to look at the multiplication.

Equation: $$q = x \cdot y$$

Autograd wants to test $x$, so it freezes $y$ at $3$.

It asks: "If I add exactly 1 to $x$, what happens to $q$?"

Let's test it: Since $x=2$ and $y=3$, currently $q=6$. If we bump $x$ up to $3$, the new math is $3 \cdot 3 = 9$.

The answer jumped by exactly 3! Because of the multiplier $y$, one turn of Gear X causes Gear Q to spin 3 times.

Step 4: The Math Equation

$\frac{\partial f}{\partial x} = 3$

Autograd connects the gears mathematically by multiplying the steps. This is the exact definition of the Chain Rule:

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial x} = \frac{\partial f}{\partial q} \cdot \frac{\partial q}{\partial x}$$
